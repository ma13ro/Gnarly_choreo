What is Choreo?

Choreo is a free, browser-based rhythm chart authoring tool that exports .sm files, synchronized to YouTube videos. It's a humanitarian project — built to make cardio through rhythm gaming as accessible as possible. No install, no account, no cost. Just open it and create.

by Claude & GNARLY

Finding a Song & BPM

Use Search for a YouTube video on the home screen to find a song without leaving Choreo, or paste a YouTube URL/video ID directly if you already have one. Once you've got a video and an artist/title, hit Check BPM to look it up automatically via songbpm.com — if there's more than one match, candidates appear as tappable chips so you can pick the right one.

Lookup doesn't always find a match. If it comes up empty, enter the BPM manually and use Tap Tempo to nail it down by ear instead.

Input Latency

If you use Bluetooth headphones or a Bluetooth controller, sound reaches your ears later than the system clock thinks. The calibration tool on the home page measures this gap — tap along to the metronome through your actual playback device, and Choreo will apply the offset automatically during scoring.

Gamepad Support

Choreo supports controllers via the browser Gamepad API — plug in or pair your controller and it's detected automatically. For a DualShock 4 in Singles mode, both the D-pad and face buttons (□ ✕ △ ○) map to the four lanes — use whichever feels natural. In Doubles mode the D-pad becomes the left pad (lanes 1–4) and the face buttons become the right pad (lanes 5–8), mirroring a two-pad dance setup. VS mode follows the same split during its 8-lane playback — P1 on the D-pad/left pad, P2 on face buttons/right pad. Options toggles play/pause, Share toggles record/playback. Triggers, sticks, and the PS button are unmapped and pass through to the browser normally.

Use the Test Controller page (linked from the home screen) to check your specific controller — it shows every button and axis live so you can confirm your hardware is registering correctly before you start authoring.

Player assignment normally follows connection order — the first controller Chrome sees is Player 1, the second is Player 2. On some platforms, especially Windows/Chrome, a phantom "Xbox 360 Controller" slot can appear even with nothing actually plugged in, occupying index 0 and bumping your real controller to Player 2. If your controller's D-pad or buttons seem to be firing on the wrong side in Doubles or VS mode, this is almost always why. Fix it from the Test Controller page: each connected pad has a Player Assignment dropdown that lets you manually force it to Player 1 or Player 2, overriding the automatic connection-order guess.

⚠️ Android browser controller limitations: on Android, Chrome only receives whatever input data Android's own OS-level HID layer decides to forward — a web page has no way to change or bypass this. We've confirmed that some wired and Bluetooth adapters (including generic PS2-to-USB and Wii Classic/GameCube-to-Bluetooth adapters) that work correctly on desktop browsers report only D-pad/left-stick input on Android, with face buttons, shoulder buttons, and Start/Select never reaching the browser at all — not a Choreo bug, and not fixable from any website. If your controller is missing buttons on Android but works fine on PC, this is almost certainly why.

If you need full button support on a phone or tablet, your best options are: (1) use Choreo on a desktop or laptop browser instead, where this restriction doesn't apply, or (2) use a dedicated Android controller-mapping app (search "gamepad mapper" or "controller mapper" on the Play Store) that operates below the browser and can re-expose your controller's full input to Chrome correctly. Choreo's D-pad-only fallback (see Test Controller) is designed to keep dance-pad-style play fully functional on Android even when face buttons don't come through.

Snap

Snap controls how notes are quantized to the beat grid. 1/4 snaps to quarter notes, 1/8 to eighth notes, 1/16 to sixteenth notes. Free places notes at the exact moment you press with no rounding — useful for fine adjustments but harder to keep clean.

Importing an Existing Chart

Use Import existing .sm to edit on the home screen to continue work on a chart made outside Choreo, or reopen an exported one another way. Choose a .sm or .ssc file, enter the YouTube URL for the matching video when prompted, and it opens directly in the authoring view with the existing notes and BPM markers intact. Import currently reads the dance-single chart from the file — Doubles/VS charts can be exported but not re-imported yet.

Saved Charts

Hit Save Chart in the authoring view to store your progress right in the browser — saving again on the same song updates that entry instead of duplicating it. Saved charts appear under Saved Charts on the home screen, where you can reopen (✎), re-download the .sm (⬇), or delete (✕) them. They live only on this browser and device.

Community Charts

The Community Charts hub lets you browse and share charts with everyone else, not just save them locally. Search by title, artist, or uploader, sort by 🔥 Top or 🆕 New, and vote (▲/▼) on the ones you like. Singles charts have an Open in Choreo button to jump straight into editing; every chart also has a plain ⬇ Download for dropping into your rhythm game's song folder directly.

Sharing a chart validates the file's actual content, not just its name — a real #TITLE, #BPMS, and playable #NOTES block are required, so the hub only ever holds genuine charts. There are no accounts, so "uploader" is just a display name and votes are tracked per-browser rather than per-person — good enough for a community tool, but not a strong guarantee against abuse.

Beat Offset (± ms)

The beat offset shifts where BPM markers land relative to the video. If your arrows feel consistently early or late against the music, nudge this until the markers line up with the beat visually. Found in the top bar during authoring — each press moves by 1ms:

0ms

This is different from input latency — it corrects the chart itself, not your reaction time.

Right next to the beat offset in the top bar, this scales the video from 40% up to 200%:

Zooming in crops evenly off the left and right edges — the video area itself grows taller (pushing everything below it down) so the top and bottom are never cut off.

Playback Mode (K)

Press K to toggle between Record and Playback mode — the same K button sits in the authoring controls, filled red when recording and faded when not:

Record Playback

In Record mode, pressing lane keys places notes. In Playback mode, the highway becomes interactive — hit arrows as they reach the receptor to score. Combo, accuracy, and judgements (Perfect / Great / Good / Miss) are tracked in the HUD above the highway.

Hold Notes

To place a hold, press and hold a lane key for longer than a short tap. The hold length is determined by how long you hold. In Playback mode, press when the head arrives and keep holding until the tail passes the receptor — releasing early counts as a Miss.

Pad Modes (Singles / Doubles / VS)

The heart button in the top bar cycles through three pad modes: Singles (4 lanes, one pad), Doubles (8 lanes, two pads sharing one chart), and VS (two players competing on the same chart). Switching modes clears current notes.

VS is authored just like Singles — 4 lanes, same keys — but during playback it mirrors into 8 lanes, one player on each half, each with independent scoring. Two random heart colors mark P1 and P2 in the VS HUD. Doubles exports as dance-double in the .sm file; VS exports the same way Singles does, since the underlying chart is still just 4 lanes.

Tap Tempo

The Tap button in the BPM bar lets you find the BPM of a song by feel. Play the song and tap the button in time with the beat for at least 4 beats — Choreo averages your taps and sets the BPM automatically. The longer you tap, the more accurate the result. Use this to verify a looked-up BPM or to find the tempo of a song that isn't in the database.

Once you have a BPM, hit Mark to place a BPM marker at the current playhead position. This locks the beat grid to that point in the song.

Songs with Changing BPM

Many songs shift tempo — a slower intro, a key change, or a breakdown. Choreo handles this with multiple BPM markers. Each marker tells the chart "from this point forward, the BPM is X."

To chart a tempo change: pause the video at the moment the new BPM begins, use Tap or the ± nudge buttons to dial in the new tempo, then press Mark to place the marker. Repeat for each section. The highway will reflect the new grid spacing from that marker onward.

Markers are shown as chips below the BPM bar — tap the × on any chip to remove it. The first marker always sets the song's base BPM.

Changing Lane Keys

In the authoring view, the lane key bindings are shown at the bottom of the screen as keyboard chips. Tap any key chip to enter edit mode — it turns green. Then press the new key you want to assign. Tap elsewhere to cancel. Changes are saved automatically per device.

Default singles layout: Q W E R — left, down, up, right.

Doubles adds a second pad: U I O P by default. All eight keys are individually reassignable the same way.

Exporting

Hit ⬇ Export .sm to download a compatible rhythm chart file. Drop it into your rhythm game's song folder alongside your audio file. The chart includes all BPM markers, tap notes, hold notes, offset, and metadata.
